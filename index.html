About Me

Research

During my PhD in the Carmen team, I developed immersed boundary methods for the inverse problem of EIT (Electrical Impedance Tomography), a non-invasive imaging technique aimed at reconstructing the conductivity within a given volume. For additional information on immersed boundary methods, please refer to the following:
Peskin CS. The immersed boundary method , A parallel second order cartesian method for elliptic interface problems M. Cisternino, L. Weynans

My thesis was conducted in an electrophysiological context, with a primary emphasis on exploring EIT within the realm of medical imaging. The body of interest was the torso, and the objective was to reconstruct organ conductivity. Other than the fact that EIT is a severely ill-posed inverse problem, another challenge is that we can have some lack of information regarding the shape of the domain where conductivity reconstruction is intended. Consequently, we encountered a problem involving a mobile geometry. Here are some references on the shape optimization aspect of EIT: Dardé et al. Simultaneous recovery of admittivity and body shape in electrical impedance tomography: an experimental evaluation .
In such cases, the application of immersed boundary methods can be proved advantageous as it eliminates the need for a costly remeshing step, unlike adaptive mesh methods.
